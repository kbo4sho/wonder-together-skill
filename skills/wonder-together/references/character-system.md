# Wonder Together Character System

Use this reference when a request needs consistent characters across Wonder
Together visuals or site graphics.

## Canonical Pair

Working labels:

- `Big Wonderer`
- `Little Wonderer`

Their canonical look is the **Pebble Guides** direction: gender-neutral,
nonhuman, rounded parent-child diagram actors. They are relational anchors, not
fixed human characters, brand mascots, literal bears, teddy toys, or animal
mascots. Tiny bear-adjacent cues are allowed only when they stay quiet: ear nubs,
rounded posture, and a subtle nose dash.

## Design Principles

- Explanation-first: the child question, concept, object, map, path, or activity
  stays more important than the characters.
- Minimal diagram-actor style: recognizable through silhouette, scale, posture,
  and shared attention.
- Warm but restrained: enough parent-child trust for a shared moment, simple
  enough for diagrams, prompts, and small site graphics.
- Site graphics should use them as quiet anchors in examples, spot moments, and
  shared-looking poses, not as character-forward brand leads.
- Use the moon explainer's pebble-like pair as the stronger reference. Avoid
  drifting toward full bear-family character-sheet styling for production site
  graphics.

## Silhouette And Proportion

Big Wonderer:

- About 1.4 to 1.7 times Little Wonderer's height.
- Taller, broader rounded capsule or pebble body with a slightly settled base.
- Tiny rounded ear nubs or softened corner-ear shapes, integrated into the
  head/body silhouette.
- Tiny dot eyes and one subtle nose dash or very small muzzle mark.
- Short guiding arms with mitten-like simplicity when a gesture is needed.
- Calm, steady posture, often leaning slightly toward Little Wonderer.

Little Wonderer:

- Smaller, lighter rounded pebble body with a slightly lifted curious posture.
- Ear nubs echo Big Wonderer but stay smaller and simpler.
- Tiny dot eyes angled toward the concept being explored.
- One subtle nose dash or tiny muzzle mark matching Big Wonderer.
- Optional single repeatable mark only when needed for readability; avoid
  clothing as identity.
- Often closer to the thing being wondered about.

Together:

- Show relationship through scale, proximity, shared gaze, and protective
  spacing.
- Big Wonderer usually stands beside or slightly behind Little Wonderer, never
  looming over the scene.
- Little Wonderer usually faces the concept; Big Wonderer often shares the gaze
  or gently points.
- Avoid adult/child stereotypes, family-role labels, gendered clothing,
  eyelashes, hair styles, or human-specific identity markers.

## Palette And Linework

- Hand-drawn dark ink linework, slightly imperfect but clear.
- Warm cream paper, warm gray, tan, and other low-saturation field-guide fills.
- Character fills stay neutral and quiet; use stronger color on the explainer
  layer instead: arrows, sightlines, paths, labels, active mechanisms, contrast
  marks, and safety cues.
- Use simple fills with light paper texture when available.
- Keep face marks and limbs readable at small sizes.

## Default Poses

- Shared looking: Big beside or behind Little, both looking at the concept.
- Gentle pointing: one short arm from Big or Little points toward the important
  mechanism.
- Protective beside: Big slightly behind Little, close enough to signal safety
  without enclosing them.
- Activity setup: Little nearer to the prop or drawing, Big beside them as the
  adult guide.
- Quiet symbolic presence: for tender topics only when a nonliteral parent-child
  presence helps; otherwise omit them.

## Usage Rules

- Use the pair to anchor attention, scale, or emotional safety.
- Keep science, history, body, map, activity, or object content as the main
  subject.
- In diagrams and site graphics, make the pair small and clear rather than
  expressive and central.
- Reuse the same silhouettes, face marks, relative scale, and neutral fills
  across an image set.
- Do not use them in literal grief, illness, medical, violent, or frightening
  scenes unless the image is symbolic and gentle.

## Avoid

- Xiaohei-style blob characters or direct imitation of any existing blob-figure
  identity.
- A cute mascot narrator who replaces the parent-child practice.
- Literal bear anatomy, realistic fur, claws, paw pads, tails, animal costumes,
  teddy-bear stitching, plush-toy seams, or toy-like button eyes.
- Prominent snouts, full bear feet, detailed paws, walking-animal anatomy, or
  poses that read as a bear family instead of quiet pebble guides.
- Gendered names, clothing, hair, eyelashes, or adult/child costume cues.
- Brand posters, title cards, product shots, sticker poses, or character
  merchandise poses.

## Prompt Snippet

Use this wording when asking for generated visuals:

`Include the Wonder Together recurring pair: Big Wonderer and Little Wonderer in the Pebble Guides style. They are gender-neutral, nonhuman minimal diagram actors with rounded pebble/capsule silhouettes, tiny rounded ear nubs, tiny dot eyes, a subtle nose dash or very small muzzle mark, short mitten-like arms when needed, dark hand-drawn linework, and quiet warm cream/warm-gray/tan field-guide fills. Big Wonderer is taller, broader, steady, and usually beside or slightly behind Little Wonderer. Little Wonderer is smaller, curious, and looking toward the concept. Keep the pair visually quiet and explanation-first; reserve stronger color for arrows, sightlines, paths, labels, active mechanisms, contrast marks, and safety cues. Do not make them mascots, humans, literal bears, teddy toys, animal characters, blob-character copies, posters, title-card figures, sticker poses, or product graphics.`
